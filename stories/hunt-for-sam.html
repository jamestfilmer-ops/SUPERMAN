The Hunt for Sam "Lead Mouth" Rockwell

Story  ·  Loading...

This little town was your honey hole for sin. If you believe in that sort of thing. It had nothing really goin' for it other than a man could ride in on Friday, whore around and sip whiskey that night through Sunday mornin' before draggin' himself to the small little box of pews on the outskirt of town, where some high-horse rider told the townsfolk to repent and be good people. I ne'r really did go to that. Reckoned I wasn't a bad enough person.

Well anyhow, there was a few years ago when that sort of man — the one with the rags and fancy hat — came in on his way out west with a dark glint in his eye. I remember I sat on my porch in front of my jail watchin' the sand crawl. It was about midday or so. I saw him tie up his horse and rent a room at Sally's Inn.

That man shot me a glance not too far from where we're sittin', just over in front of the inn as he tied up his horse to the trough. A beautiful red one, too. Made me feel jealous for a second even. The look, it was quick. I don't even know if he saw me look back it was so quick, and I had my hat over my head, so I do reckon he never even saw my face, but I saw his.

"My heart was warnin' me this man was danger."

We got a lot of strange characters from time to time, and I'm sure a few criminals went under my notice. This man he stuck out to me, though. I don't oft' drink, but I get angry when I do. Well, I was dead sober when that glint reached me over here, but I felt that same spike of anger in ma' heart when he done it, looked over here. My heart was warnin' me this man was danger.

That night, I awoke from my cot to screamin' and yellin'. I walked out in my undergarments they was so hysterical. I smelled smoke before long. Walkin' up the street, I saw Sally's Inn was ablaze and that man's red horse was nowhere to be seen. I'll save you the details from what we found at Sally's. I can tell you it was so bad I sat in one them pews that Sunday.

As I left, I done got this feelin'. Preacher Man talked about this guy named Moses who talked with God. When I left that steeple my bones were on fire and my lips, doggone my lips were curled. I hadn't cried in who knows how long, but a tear shed on my cheek as I walked back home.

I don't reckon I had much stuff. Some clothes and a gun and a hat. I stuffed what cash I did have in my pocket and shut that door. I gave the badge and the keys to the cells to Benjamin at the bar. Told him not to let it burn down when I got back. He asked where I was goin', I told him straight. There was a devil out there and I aimed to shoot him. He said he'd come with me. So I told him 'leave the key with your brother' and he said, 'Alright.'

Within the hour we'd our horses and stood at the edge of town. I couldn't help feelin' scared. Side by side, I noticed my horse was pretty tall. A bit taller than his. Maybe that's what you call irony. Anyhow, we left at sundown, headed west. The horses started to gallop, and I remember thinkin' I was too damn old to fall off.

· · ·

We rode out till the sun cracked down below the mountains, about eight miles or so. Sleep was immediate. No need for any fire. My guess was he was a coward. I had a few bits of evidence for that, and Benjamin agreed. First off, only fools and cowards hurt a woman. They can't really fight back. Cowards go for them to feel big. That much I do know. He also ran.

Well, I guess now I was the judge out here.

· · ·

By nightfall we had reached a little local hole of a town. Asked around. Benjamin been right about goin' Southwest. They said they seen the man too, but he hadn't stayed a whole night. Stopped in for a drink and some cards before leavin' but not before he got in a scuffle with a man about his wife's honor.

Well, we at least knew what he looked like a bit better than we had before leavin'. Blue eyes, short black hair but a nice clean-shaven face. And we had a name. Sam. Sam Rockwell.

· · ·

Years ago, I called him a coward. But he was standin' before me, that sun beatin' behind him but in front was this fog of sand and dirt. He told me why he done that terrible thing to them women. The devil wasn't to blame nor were his parents or his father beatin' him or God's wrath or nothin'. He just wanted to. He said he chose evil because it was more fun.

I can't believe I was able to lift that gun with how heavy my heart felt after hearin' that. My shoulders just about broke but my arm kept movin'. He did the same. This heavy boom rushed through my chest, and I fell to my feet. The shot wasn't what hurt, I guess. It was the feeling of losing. He came over to stand up before me, presumably to shoot my brains out.

Benjamin laid him dead from the window above the street.
